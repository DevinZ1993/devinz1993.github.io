The Economist - 05/09/2020


This post is what I learned from the recent issue of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




Santa Cruz, 05/10/2020


A dangerous gap

  • Even with all the dramatic precedents in stock-market history, the past eight weeks still have been remarkable.
    • The S&P 500 lost a third of its value in a month right before it rocketed and recovered half of the loss.
  • The rosy view from Wall Street contrasts life on Main Street, where unemployment is still savage.
  • The optimistic mood is because of the Fed, who has relentless bought up corporate bonds to avoid a deeper recession.
  • The market was lopsided to a handful of businesses even before the pandemic, and it has become even more so.
  • There are countless threats to the sanguine prospect.
    • There is the risk of an aftershock, such as a second wave of infections or a steep recession.
    • The market's confidence is vulnerable to fraud scandals bred over the years of the expansion.
    • The increasing gap between smaller firms and bigger corporations may lead to a political backlash.
  • A one-month bear market scarcely seems enough time to absorb all the possible bad news from the pandemic and the huge uncertainty it has created.

The food miracle

  • The food miracle reflects a $8trn global supply chain, which is an unsung star of 21st-century logistics.
  • Concentration and government intervention, along with the vagaries of the climate and commodity markets, have resulted devastating consequences in history (2007-08).
  • The global food system has so far passed the server test, with each of its layer adapted.
  • The ever-present risk is that rising poverty or production glitches will lead panicky politicians to stockpile food and limit exports.
  • Governments need to hold their nerve and keep the world's food system open for business.
  • The food security relies on interdependence and diversity, rather than self-sufficiency drives and blunt intervention.

The pandemic is a turning point in history

  • The covid-19 is a pivotal that challenges the old order, forcing questions about what sort of future we want, what the proper role of government is and what makes a healthy society.
  • Long before covid-19, there were warnings about deep social inequalities and the shortcomings of globalization.
  • As history shows, those societies that survive and adapt best to catastrophes are already strong.
    • Some countries controlled the pandemic more effectively because people have faith in the authorities and each other.
  • Much also depends on leaders. Wise leaders in the past have been able to steer away from danger.
  • The present crisis could be the opportunity for strategies to produce essential public goods and ensure that citizens have safe, decent and fulfilled lives.

Parallel universe

  • Through aggressive use of economic sanctions, America is misusing its clout as the predominant financial power, thereby pushing allies and foes alike towards building a separate financial architecture.
  • America's pull within the system remains huge, and the world's financial plumbing remains under its thumb.
  • Three things are driving the change.
    • America has started to cripple rivals by denying them access to the world's liquidity supply, which creates a strong incentive for others to seek workarounds.
    • Many emerging markets, not only China, are keen on a rebalance to meet their financial needs.
    • The covid-19 further fragments global trade and could dent confidence on America's ability to guarantee global prosperity.
