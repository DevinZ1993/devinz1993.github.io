Golden Gate Bridge - 08/16/2020


The exception

  • As the rupture between China and America in tech industry continues to grow, the pattern in high finance, however, is of superpower engagement, not estrangement.
  • Although western, and in particular American, capital markets still reign supreme on most measures, Wall Street's taste for China reflects a long-term bet that finance's center of gravity will shift east.
  • The rush of Wall Street firms and other foreign investors into China has potentially great economic interests but also some political risks.
  • The trade war has shown the clout of China's capital markets, and has also given the control-obsessed officials an incentive to loosen their grip further.

Office politics

  • People have drawn radically different conclusions on the future of the office, with both optimism and worries.
  • Built since the age of steam power, the system of central offices always had glaring shortcomings, some of which have become worse over time.
  • Before covid-19, the inertia allowed the office to escape serious disruption of the technologies of the recent decades.
  • Since the pandemic, a powerful network effect has happened on remote-working technologies, and bureaucratic hurdles to remote work have been blasted out of the way.
  • Optional offices might be the new norm when a vaccine arrives, but the exact balance will depend on the industry and city.
  • Companies will have to adapt to the pattern of sporadic attendance with more targeted staff interactions.
  • Governments had better to embrace the consequences of the technological change by modernizing employment law and redeveloping city centers.

Power in the 21st century

  • As the public governments and investors wake up to climate change, the clean-energy industry is gaining momentum.
  • Compared with fossil fuels, the emerging new energy system will not only be better to the environment and human health, but should also be more politically stable and less economically volatile.
  • Two risks stand out:
    • Autocratic China could temporarily gain clout over the global power system.
    • Petrostates will face a vicious fight for market share, without enough public resources for economic and political reform.
  • The world's move to a new energy order is vital, but it will be messy.

Why governments get it wrong

  • The increase in diagnosed cases reflects not only the gloomy reality but also extra testing.
  • Treatments and medicines have brought down the fatalities, and more progress is in store.
  • The pandemic will remain a part of daily life well into 2021.
  • Governments need to identify a reasonable trade-off between shutting down to keep people alive and staying open so that life goes on.
  • Governments like America, Britain, Israel and Spain persist in getting it disastrously wrong.
