The Economist - 05/02/2020


This post is what I learned from the recent issue of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




San Francisco, 12/28/2019


The 90% economy

  • An 90% economy is miserable as shown in China, and probably also presage a hard life in the post-lockdown rich world.
  • Economies will be held back even if restrictions are sensibly eased.
    • Leaving lockdown is a process, not an event.
    • The uncertainty will inhibit those who fear the disease.
  • Many business will emerge from lockdown short of money, which will further deepen people's financial worries.
  • A deep, long recession will stoke anger and summon popular demand for political changes, either constructive or bleak.

Unsteady states

  • The pandemic is a budgetary cataclysm to American's states, counties and cities, whose rainy-day funds are insufficient and unevenly distributed. Infusions from the federal government are therefore the only way to avoid ill-timed austerity.
  • Help from Congress has so far been lacking, for the reason that it would be costly to create the impression that Congress will, in a downturn, bail out profligate states and cities.
  • The pandemic is a natural disaster in which Americans should share risk as in the past. Besides, making up tax shortfalls during lockdowns would benefit all states.
  • The Fed's loans are both inadequate due to restrictions on borrowing, and undesirable for the political risks and moral hazard it may introduce. Thus, better for Congress to stump up.

West-coast shuffle

  • Tech firms are thriving amid a savage recession, compared with more traditional big firms that are enduring the pain.
  • Hints of vulnerability are shown in advertising revenue, smartphone sales and services relying on human contact.
  • New sources of growth are becoming clearer, potentially shifting tech's spiritual center of gravity from San Francisco to Seattle.

Open schools first

  • Shutting schools and keeping children at home is imposing heavy costs on children's development, on their parents and on the economy.
    • Real-life teachers and the social skills acquired in the playground are irreplaceable.
    • Without schools helping level the playing field, the achievement gap between affluent and working-class children will grow.
    • Parents who work at home are less productive if distracted by young children, and mothers having to carry out child care may lose ground in the workplace.
  • Children are not as likely to catch or die from the disease as adults, and there's no evidence that they could be silent spreaders who pass it on to their families.
  • Schools should re-open in stages, starting from youngest children and then those facing exams.
  • Making school attendance voluntary may entrench educational inequality. Instead, governments should insist that education is compulsory, but don't fine frightened parents willy-nilly.
