Cookie the Great, 07/14/2019


The business of survival

  • The crisis is set to amplify three trends:
    • a quicker adoption of new technologies;
    • an inevitable retreat from freewheeling global supply chains;
    • a worrying rise in corporate concentration and cronyism.
  • Numerous indicators suggest extreme stress in commercial activity, even though many firms are putting a brave face on it.
  • The pain will deepen as defaults cascade through domestic payment chains and global supply chains.
  • Not all firms can survive the recession. Which will do depends on their industry, their balance-sheets and how easily they can tap government loans, guarantees and aid.
  • In an intermediate phase after the lockdown, ingenuity will become a source of advantage, allowing clever firms to operate closer to full speed.

Cut to the chase

  • Europe is repeating its history in 2010-12: talks over the covid-19 crisis has descended into another ugly row over which countries gain and lose from a common currency.
  • Southern countries need government spending to avoid a deep and enduring slump, but that will sharply increase their debts.
  • Northerners have long resisted mutualisation for fear of underwriting laxity in the south.
  • The currency union cannot succeed unless its members share more risk. Failing to face this may sabotage the survival of the euro and perhaps the EU itself.

Wisconsin's warning

  • Wisconsin, as a purple state that is bitterly fought over in national elections, has just provided a lesson how not to run an election.
  • In any democracy, a party that considers pursuing a lower turnout to be a legitimate electoral strategy does not deserve to win elections.
  • Partisanship, left unchecked, has shifted the struggle from policy to the hackery manipulation of the rules.
  • States must not dither. Either make it easier to vote safely in person, or find secure ways to vote remotely.

5Geopolitics

  • Two rival factions in the 1990s' American telecoms industry:
    • "bellheads" believe in proprietary technology, vertical monopolies and deference to regulators;
    • "netheads" favor open-source software, collaboration between firms, and decentralized decision-making.
  • Earlier iterations of mobile technology all had their winners. Clearly, America does not want the winner of this time to be China.
  • 5G will not profoundly alter consumers' lives, but it enables networks that can support the "Internet of Things".
  • The reason why Western security hawks fear Huawei is less that Huawei hoovers up data. Rather, in a conflict between China and the West, the firm could shut down an enemy's network and maybe even turn 10T devices into weapons.
  • America's tough talk has had limited success and even backfired.
    • American tech companies found legal loopholes to keep selling to Huawei.
    • Only three allies complied with banishment against Huawei.
    • It also prompted Huawei to wean itself off American technology.
  • The open-architecture virtualized network launched by Rakuten suggests a nethead alternative.

Strategic pile-up

  • The virus has reinforced an old idea: some industries are "strategic", simply too important for countries to leave to unfettered markets, and so deserving of special protection.
  • In reality, no one can agree on which industries count as "strategic".
  • Governments should resist indulging firms too liberally.
    • Sheltering them behind national-security arguments is, like all protectionism, expensive.
    • It risks ushering in the baleful logic of the prisoner's dilemma and leaving the world divided in the face of the next crisis.

Stacked and whacked

  • Startup founders are giving investors preferred stocks with liquidation preference in exchange for higher "post-money" valuations.
  • Funds raised at lofty values can create misalignment later on between founders, early-stage vcs and late-stage investors.
