The Economist - 06/13/2020


This post is what I learned from the recent issue of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




University of Washington, 07/08/2017


The power of protest

  • The legacy of George Floyd, the rich promise of social reform, is too precious to waste.
  • The protest may be accounted for by the sheer ubiquity of social media, the pandemic that coops people up, and a president who sets out to sow division, but most fundamentally it reflects a rising rejection of racism itself.
  • America is not only a country, but also an idea, which wraps up a conviction that progress is possible.
  • The progress is happening in three ways:
    • Both democratic and republican politicians are pushing forward police reforms.
    • Federal government is making efforts to reduce racial segregation.
    • The business world has an incentive to improve diversity for higher profit margins.
  • Protest movements have a habit of antagonizing the moderate supporters that large-scale social change needs to succeed.

Achilles heal

  • The miserable mood of many economic forecasters is in striking contrast with booming stockmarkets and the increasingly bullish mood in many boardrooms, making some believe this is a clear sign of spreading irrational exuberance.
  • A stream of positive corporate and economic data provides some grounds for optimism, both in America and around the globe.
  • Governments' stimuli have successfully shielded firms from bankruptcy and protected consumers' incomes.
  • Yet the rally is prone to both the withdrawal of stimulus and a second wave of pandemic.

After disaster

  • Covid-19 struck the most exciting, global cities hardest, with appalling death tolls and grave quietness. It has attacked the core of what makes large western cities vibrant and successful.
  • Losing people and business may forebode a fiscal crunch of cities, and even a spiral of budget cuts, deteriorating services, rising crime and middle-class flight.
  • Cities remain invaluable as places where people can build networks and learn how to collaborate, but they need more automony to adapt to the post-covid era.
  • Cities are not only big contributors of national economy, but also where people learn to live in a modern, open society.

Reality check

  • Although machine learning has recently made much progress, doubts have lately been creeping in about whether today's AI technology is really as world-changing as it seems.
  • Just as the former two waves of AI-related excitement, many of the grandest claims made about AI have once again failed to become reality.
  • Those hoping to make use of AI's potential must confront two sets of problems.
    • The massive data and computational power that machine learning demands can be impractical.
    • Machine learning algorithms only excel at well-bounded tasks, but can get things very wrong if faced with unexpected input.
  • Another full-blown AI winter is unlikely, but an autumnal breeze is picking up.
