The Economist - August 2020


This post is what I learned from recent issues of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




Memorial Church, 07/22/2020


How to cope with middle age

  • Google is in the midst of a mid-life crisis despite its glorious success on the surface today. This is what its founders would never embrace.
  • The signs of ageing are apparent in Google's maturing business, its changing culture and its ever-more-entwined relationship with government.
    • Unearthing other gold mines has proved difficult.
    • The freewheeling ethos has become a liability.
    • The attention of trustbusters has long looked inevitable.
  • Should Google put its faith in managers, investors or geeks in response to the crisis?
    • GE's woes offer a warning of the peril of relying on one hugely successful division (GE Capital) to subsidize less profitable units elsewhere.
    • Spinning off YouTube seems profitable, but the experiences of firms like AT&T and IBM highlight the danger that downsizing hollows out innovation.
    • Both Microsoft and Apple bounced back by rediscovering their core purpose and applying it in a new way.
  • Indiscipline (as what Google does today) can lead to unexpected innovations, but more often saps vitality.

Trumpian TikTok

  • The Sino-American tech tensions now culminate in a forced sale of TikTok's American business to a domestic buyer.
  • ByteDance is the latest in a series of Chinese firms to be targeted by the Trump administration amid fears of cyber-spying and propaganda-peddling, and will not be the last in the crosshairs.
  • The data threat from China is real, but the expropriation detached from any rules or procedure undermines investor confidence in America and heaps political pressure on technocratic bodies like CFIUS.
  • CFIUS probes need to conclude more promptly, and the committee's political independence also needs bolstering.
  • A new federal data-privacy and cyber-security law would create transparent standards for handling America's data and stymie foreign hackers.

Xi's new economy

  • The Trump administration has assumed that China's steroidal state capitalism is weaker than it looks and thus pressing hard enough will lead to China's concessions and even collapse.
  • China's economy has shown to be more resilient than expected and could propel growth for years.
  • Underestimating China's economy is hardly a new phenomenon.
  • Xi has started the next phase of Chinese state capitalism to increase order and resilience against threats for good reasons.
  • Xinomics has three elements:
    • Tight control over the economic and debt machine.
    • A more efficient administrative state, whose rules apply uniformly across the economy.
    • A blurred boundary between state and private firms.
  • Xinomics has performed well in the short term, but history suggests that diffuse decision-making, open borders and free speech are the magic ingredients for sustained innovation.
  • The hope for confrontation followed by capitulation is misguided.

Silicon Valley v Wall Street

  • A shift in the balance of power towards tech startups has caused a striking decline of IPOs in the past two decades.
  • IPOs brokered by Wall Street banks tend to be gruelling and expensive ordeals for firms.
  • Direct listing and blank-cheque are alternatives to IPOs, but they both have drawbacks.
  • The IPO process needs improvement, but companies that take advantage of frothy stockmarkets will eventually get hammered.
