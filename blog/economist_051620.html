The Economist - 05/16/2020


This post is what I learned from the recent issue of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




West Seattle, 05/24/2018


Goodbye globalisation

  • Globalization has suffered from three body-blows: the 2018 financial crisis, the sino-American trade war, and the global pandemic of covid-19.
  • With increasing international disputes, the underlying anarchy of global governance is being exposed.
  • The international movement of people (travel and immigration), goods (trade) and capital (investment) is suffering.
  • Domesticating supply chains will risk and forfeit economies of scale, and a fractured world will make solving global problems harder.

On the blink

  • The EU has muddled through since its early days, although the journey towards integration has been bumpy.
  • The pandemic is becoming a political and constitutional crisis in Europe, for the EU's members cannot agree on what is needed to make their union more resilient, nor on how to bring about reform.
  • Three bonds of membership are being tested: the single market, the single currency, and the status of EU law.
  • The EU should be more aggressive, which means overcoming the taboo against treaty change, and adopting bigger transfers and significant debt mutualization.

Reopen and shut

  • Never before have governments erected safety-nets as generous as those they have created during the pandemic.
  • The emergency policies are indispensable, but maintaining them too long would be ruinously expensive and prevent labour markets from adapting to the new way of life that emerges from the pandemic.
  • It seems increasingly likely that economies will be permanently changed, in terms of consumer habits, employee demands and industries' hiring preferences.
  • The governments support would prevent the necessary adjustments from taking place, but withdrawing it too early would leave legions of unemployment workers fending for themselves in brutal conditions.
  • How to face the difficult balancing art?
    • The most lavish support should be maintained only in industries which the government is forcibly keeping closed.
    • Schemes should encourage flexibility.
    • Governments should help people find new jobs.

The next garage

  • Definitive layoff figures in tech's heartland are hard to come by, as many sackings at smaller startups are inevitably missed.
  • VCs are scouting for promising firms whose valuations have dropped and which need fresh capital, while startups with cash in the coffers are seizing the opportunity to scoop up weaker rivals.
  • Venture capital is flowing to offerings for business that are delivered virtually and targets that are deeply technical (Confluent).
  • The crisis will accelerate the existing trend of exodus, which has been driven by exorbitant property prices, near-permanent traffic jams and the jarring number of homeless people.
  • Another question is whether venture capital, Silicon Valley's lifeblood, will go virtual and distributed as well.
