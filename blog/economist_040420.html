The Economist - 04/04/2020


This post is what I learned from the recent issue of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




Cookie the Great, 01/05/2019


A grim calculus

  • Covid-19 presents stark and diehard choices to governments. Leaders have to "put a dollar figure on life".
  • As the pandemic ravages the world, nations are making trade-offs in different ways. Not all of them make sense.
  • Three principles of thinking about these trade-offs:
    • be systematic (contending moral and social values);
    • help those on the losing side (sacked workers and children);
    • adapt to changes (in case of a gloomy future).

Bottomless Pit, Inc

  • Governments are giving unprecedented handouts to help private firms live through the lockdowns, which is necessary but may be toxic to the economy's dynamism.
  • Since the duration of lockdowns is unclear, private investors are reluctant to swoop in.
  • Governments should offer support for business in an integrated way. For example, make sure banks have the resources to lend so that small firms are able to get cash.
  • The cost of extending unlimited credit to all firms is unsustainable and the economy must eventually adjust to new circumstances.
  • Governments should not interfere in other ways, except banning firms getting bespoke deals from paying cash to shareholders.

Don't waste a good crisis

  • The pandemic is a good time for tech giants to detoxify their testy relations with society.
  • Big tech firms have enjoyed surging demand, and have also exhibited outstanding financial reliability.
  • Small firms are being crushed and are likely to be snapped up by the tech giants.
  • Big tech firms are acting as vital utilities in the crisis, but that also justifies regulations to cap their prices and profits.

Exit unicorns, pursued by bears

  • The perpetually loss-making business models and exuberant valuations of unicorns are increasingly being questioned. "Fear of missing out" is giving way to "fear of looking stupid".
  • The past decade has seen a huge amount of money flowing towards cash-burning firms, but the euphoria began to ebb last year. A notorious example is the fiasco of WeWork.
  • The coronavirus shock comes at a time when most tech unicorns were already exhibiting underlying health problems.
  • In the physical world, the flywheels (of Uber) gum up due to the friction of scaling costs.
  • Some believe real tech companies must be those whose products are technologies (e.g. cloud-based corp services), rather than those using technologies. But that doesn't mean consumer Internet is dead (e.g. Airbnb and ByteDance).
  • The overstated valuations are also led by the complex and opaque financial practices behind the calculation of valuations, like ownership structure and governance.
  • Firms are finding ways out including retrenchment, consolidation and acquisition (selling to the big uglies). Whatever the results, the term "unicorn" will probably no more exist.
