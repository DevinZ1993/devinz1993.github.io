The Economist - July 2020


This post is what I learned from recent issues of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




The Seattle Great Wheel, 07/24/2017


Retro or radical?

  • Thanks to Mr Trump's flailing, Mr Biden finds himself in landslide territory without having to do much to get there, and could have the opportunity to do something bigger than what was expected.
  • Change in America is made by the extremes, but those who frighten the voters cannot make lasting change through the federal government.
  • Despite being viewed with suspicion on the left, Mr Biden's caution could open up the possibility of more change than a real radical would.
  • In contrast with the past, the centrist virtues of decency, experience and a willingness to act on advice from competent people could seem more alluring in 2020.
  • A public option in health care, greenhouse-gas reduction, and return to multilateral engagement in foreign policy are in Mr Biden's policy plans.

Techtonic plates

  • The global tech industry has been enjoying a golden era despite the gloomy predictions due to tensions between America and China, but the events in the past two weeks signal that the split is about to come.
  • The American and Chinese software and internet universes, which were never particularly connected, are heading at light-speed towards total separation.
  • Hardware is moving much more slowly, because it is more globally integrated and will be expensive to split.
  • It will be surprising to see how the two technospheres of influence are drawn and how much the split could cost.

Trade without trust

  • The Huawei ban has shown that trust in the global trading system has collapsed and a new trade architecture is needed.
  • The middle way between a naive embrace of Chinese state capitalism and a cold war has proved untenable.
  • The true burden of ripping Huawei out of European networks stems from the decay of the world's trading system, and it may feed a spiralling sense of lawlessness.
  • Such disengagement and containment cannot be applied across the entire economic relationship because of China's economic status. Instead a new trade regime is needed that acknowledges China's nature.
  • The goal of the new architecture should be to maximize trade consistent with both sides' strategic security.
  • Chinese firms should also be required to accept open governance of their big subsidiaries in the West.
  • The West should act together to find a way to do business with an authoritarian state it mistrusts.

Free money

  • The covid-19 pandemic has led to a supersized level of state intervention in the economy and financial markets, and as a result a profound shift in economics will take place.
  • The four defining features of the new epoch:
    • Governments are borrowing at a jaw-dropping scale.
    • Central banks have created an enormous amount of reserves to finance the government stimulus.
    • The Fed and Treasury have waded into financial markets, actively backstopping the private sector.
    • Low inflation means no immediate need to worry about public debt.
  • The new era of economics reflects the culmination of long-term trends and may last for decades.
  • A state with a permanently broader and deeper reach across economy creates some opportunities, but also presents grave risks.
  • Sprawling macroeconomic management leads to infinite opportunities for politicians to play favorites, and further leads to distorted markets, moral hazard and low growth. Yet the single, simple tool of independent central banks -- interest rates -- seems impotent today.
  • Policymakers today needs to create a framework that allows the business cycle to be managed and financial crises to be fought without a politicized takeover of the economy.
