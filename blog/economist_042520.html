The Economist - 04/25/20


This post is what I learned from the recent issue of The Economist and DOES NOT represent my personal stand.




San Francisco, 12/28/2019


After the disease, the debt

  • Public borrowing in the rich world is set to soar to levels last seen amid the rubble and smoke of 1945 and will carry long-lasting consequences.
  • High as the national debt is, the cost of servicing it may not be demanding as long as interest rates are low.
  • Governments have to walk a treacherous path between stimulus today and prudence tomorrow.
    • Inadequate fiscal support today risks pushing the economy into a spiral of decline.
    • Wild borrowing for years would eventually threaten trouble, especially for rich countries other than America.
    • Once the virus abates, a burst of demand may boost interest rates even though they are comfortably low today.
    • The politics of deficit reduction will be toxic, especially when the pandemic calls for lavish spending.
    • In the throes of the pandemic, the withdrawal of emergency support would be self-defeating.
  • Modestly higher inflation would help ease debt burdens and meanwhile make up the shortfall of the targets.
  • Governments should raise taxes on land, inheritance, carbon emissions and, in America, consumption.

Undercut

  • Governments may wish to pay off debt with taxation.
    • One likely constraint is politics in that quite a few people would be upset for raising taxes or cutting spendings.
    • Such austerity seems to be scant and emotional.
  • Governments could default or restructure debts.
    • A modern economy integrated into global financial markets has a huge problem if capital markets lock it out.
    • Promises to increase health-care and pension spending in coming decades are easier to renege on.
  • Governments can wait it out, rolling over their debts while hoping they shrink relative to the economy over time.
    • The secret is to ensure that the economy's combined level of real economical growth and inflation stays handily above the interest rate the government pays on its debt.
    • Stopping interest rates from following inflation upwards was achieved by means of a regulatory system, which deprives citizens of better investment options and thus forces them to lend to governments at low interest rates.

A pandemic of power grabs

  • Many governments have enacted emergency laws vesting extra powers in the executive to cope with covid-19, but some of them are not necessary and won't be relinquished after the pandemic.
  • Autocrats are delighted to have respectable excuses to ban mass protests, postpone elections, selectively enforce lockdown rules, invade individual privacy, and ban "fake news".
  • Journalists and human-rights activists are not able to venture out, leaving power grabbers unchecked.
  • Many of the liberty-constricting actions taken by regimes are more detrimental to public health.
  • Autocrats' inability to deal with popular suffering will eventually cause their regimes to lose control.

Pimp the ride

  • Investors were pessimistic about he car industry even before the recession for their to-be-obsolete technologies and collapsing sales.
  • Car firms are burning cash on their high fixed costs, and may also suffer from defaults on car loans and a permanent fall in commuting.
  • Millions of jobs would be at risk if the car industry was destructed. But there is also a risk that government aid ossifies car firms before they have modernized.
  • The industry should take control of its own fate by:
    • adapting to new health protocols,
    • keeping up investments on new technologies, and
    • joining forces (consolidation).

Tear down this wall

  • Microsoft, a byword for technical walled garden two decades ago, recently joined a fledgling movement to liberate the world's data.
  • Many governments have recognized the potential of data sharing, but companies were concerned about intellectual property, users' privacy and technical hurdles.
  • Microsoft's campaign is the most consequential by far, and could be compared as IBM's embracement of Linux in the late 1990s.
  • Brad Smith believes that more sharing would counteract the concentration of economic and political power, but won't be easy.
  • Microsoft could benefit by championing open data by virtue of its business model. Unfortunately, that is not the case for many other data-rich firms.
